A
Absolute Magnitude M [dimensionless]
The apparent magnitude a celestial object would have if it were located at a standard distance of 10 parsecs from Earth. A measure of intrinsic brightness.
See also: Apparent Magnitude, Luminosity, Parallax.
Absolute Zero of Temperature [K]
Temperature at which a gas would exert no pressure.
Absorbed Dose (D) D [Gy or J kg⁻¹] HL
Total energy absorbed per unit mass of tissue.
Absorption and Emission Lines
Discrete wavelengths in a spectrum where light is absorbed or emitted by atoms as electrons transition between energy levels. Absorption lines appear as dark lines in a continuous spectrum, while emission lines appear as bright lines.
See also: Photon Emission, Transition Energy.
Acceleration (a) a [m s⁻²]
Rate of change of velocity.
See also: Velocity (u,v), Force, Newton's Second Law of Motion.
Accuracy
An indication of how close a measurement is to the accepted value (a measure of correctness).
Acoustic Impedance (Z) Z [kg m⁻² s⁻¹] HL
The product of the density of a substance and the speed of sound in that substance.
Active Solar Heater
Designed to capture as much thermal energy as possible by allowing solar radiation to heat water running through a pipe in the panel.
Activity (A) A [Bq or s⁻¹]
Number of radioactive disintegrations (decays) per unit time.
See also: Radioactive Half-life (T₁/₂), Decay Constant (λ).
Adiabatic
A process that occurs without the exchange of thermal energy (Q = 0).
Albedo (α) α [dimensionless]
Fraction of the total incoming solar radiation received by a planet that is reflected back out into space. OR Ratio of total solar radiation power scattered by a planet to total solar radiation received by a planet. Global annual mean albedo is 0.3 for Earth.
See also: Emissivity (ε), Stefan-Boltzmann Law, Greenhouse Effect.
Alpha Particle (α) α
Helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons).
See also: Radioactive Decay, Nucleon.
Amplitude A [m]
Maximum displacement from the mean position.
See also: Simple Harmonic Motion, Wave, Intensity (I).
Analog
Technique involving codes or signals that can take on a large number of different values between given limits – analog signals vary continuously with time.
See also: Digital.
Analyzer
Polarizer used to detect polarized light.
Angular Acceleration α [rad s⁻²] HL
The rate of change of angular velocity. Analogous to linear acceleration in rotational motion.
See also: Angular Speed, Torque.
Angular Momentum L [kg m² s⁻¹] HL
The rotational equivalent of linear momentum. For a rotating object, it is the product of moment of inertia and angular velocity (L = Iω).
See also: Angular Speed, Torque, Conservation of Angular Momentum.
Angular Speed ω [rad s⁻¹]
The rate of change of angular displacement. Also called angular velocity, measured in radians per second.
See also: Angular Acceleration, Tangential Speed, Period (T), Frequency (f).
Antinode
Locations of maximum constructive interference on a standing wave.
See also: Node, Standing (stationary) wave, Amplitude.
Apparent Magnitude m [dimensionless]
A measure of the brightness of a celestial object as seen from Earth. Lower values indicate brighter objects.
See also: Brightness, Absolute Magnitude, Luminosity.
Artificial (Induced) Transmutation
When a nucleus is bombarded with a nucleon, an alpha particle or another small nucleus, resulting in a nuclide with a different proton number (a different element).
Asia-Pacific Partnership on Clean Development and Climate (APPCDC)
Agreement between six countries (representing approximately 50% of the world's energy use – Australia, China, India, Japan, Republic of Korea, and USA) to work together and with private sector partners to meet goals for energy security, national air pollution reduction, and climate change in ways that promote sustainable economic growth and poverty reduction.
Atom
The smallest unit of a chemical element consisting of a nucleus surrounded by electrons.
Atomic Clock Frequency Shift Experiment
Experiment comparing two identical atomic clocks, one on the ground and one sent to a high altitude in a rocket – higher altitude clock runs faster - experimental evidence to support gravitational red-shift.
Avogadro constant (NA) NA [mol⁻¹]
The number of atoms in 0.012 kg of 12C (= 6.02 x 10²³).
B
Barometer [Pa or mmHg]
An instrument for measuring atmospheric pressure. Used in weather forecasting and altitude determination.
See also: Pressure (P).
Beta Negative Particle (β⁻) β⁻
Positron (antielectron).
See also: Radioactive Decay.
Beta Positive Particle (β⁺) β⁺
Electron.
See also: Radioactive Decay, Electron.
Binary Number
Number written using base-2.
Binding Energy BE [J or eV]
Energy released when a nuclide is assembled from its individual components. OR Energy required when nucleus is separated into its individual components.
See also: Mass Defect, Nuclear Fission, Nuclear Fusion, Nucleon.
Binding Energy per Nucleon BE/A [J or MeV]
Energy released per nucleon when a nuclide is assembled from its individual components. OR Energy required per nucleon when nucleus is separated into its individual components.
See also: Binding Energy, Mass Defect, Nucleon.
Biological Half-life (TB) TB [s] HL
Time taken for half the number of ingested radioactive nuclei in the body to be removed by natural bodily (chemical) processes.
Bit
Binary digit that can only take one of two possible values (1 or 0; ON or OFF; High or Low; True or False).
Black Holes HL
A region of spacetime with extreme curvature due to the presence of a mass.
Black-Body Radiation
Radiation emitted by a 'perfect' emitter of radiation.
Blue Giant
A massive, hot, luminous star with high surface temperature. Blue color indicates very high temperature.
See also: Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram, Luminosity.
Boiling
A phase change of a liquid into a gas that occurs at a fixed temperature.
Brightness b [W m⁻²]
The apparent intensity of light received from an astronomical object as observed from Earth. Depends on both luminosity and distance.
See also: Luminosity, Apparent Magnitude, Intensity (I), Distance.
Byte
Eight separate bits of information.
C
Capacitance (C) C [F or C V⁻¹]
Ratio of charge stored in a device to the potential difference across the device (C = q / V).
Carbon-Oxygen Burning HL
Advanced nuclear fusion stage in massive stars where carbon and oxygen nuclei fuse to create heavier elements like neon, magnesium, and silicon.
See also: Helium Burning, Nuclear Fusion.
Center of Mass rcm [m]
The point at which all the mass of an object or system can be considered to be concentrated for the purpose of analyzing translational motion.
See also: Mass, System.
Center of a Black Hole (singularity) HL
The single point to which all mass would collapse.
Centripetal Acceleration ac [m s⁻²]
The acceleration directed toward the center of circular motion, perpendicular to the velocity. Given by a = v²/r.
See also: Tangential Speed, Orbital Speed, Force.
Chain Reaction
Neutrons released from one fission reaction go on to initiate further reactions. Only low-energy neutrons (≈ 1 eV) favor nuclear fission.
Change in Gravitational Potential Energy ΔEP [J]
Product of an object's mass times the gravitational field strength times the change in height.
Charge Q or q [C]
A fundamental property of matter that causes electromagnetic interactions, measured in coulombs (C).
Circuit
A closed conducting path through which electric current can flow.
Compression
Area of high pressure in a longitudinal wave.
See also: Rarefaction, Longitudinal Wave, Pressure (P).
Conductor
Material through with electric charge flows freely.
See also: Insulator, Electric Current (I), Resistance (R).
Conservation of Angular Momentum HL
In the absence of external torques, the total angular momentum of a system remains constant.
See also: Angular Momentum, Torque.
Constructive Interference
Superposition of two waves which are in phase with each other.
See also: Destructive Interference, Principle of Superposition, Path Difference.
Continuous Progressive (Traveling) Wave
Series of periodic pulses (involves a transfer of energy). Each point on the wave has the same amplitude.
Control Rods
Are used to remove any excess neutrons to ensure the fission reaction continues safely (e.g., may be made of cadmium or boron steel).
Controlled Nuclear Fission
Used for power production.
See also: Uncontrolled Nuclear Fission, Nuclear Fission, Chain Reaction.
Coulomb Force FC [N]
The electrostatic force between charged particles. An attractive force between opposite charges and a repulsive force between like charges.
See also: Electric Field Strength (E), Charge, Coulomb's Law, Strong Force.
Coulomb interaction
Electrostatic force of repulsion between the protons in the nucleus.
Coulomb's Law
The electric force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the two charges and inversely proportional to square of the distance between them, and directed along the line joining the two charges (F = k q₁ q₂ / r²).
See also: Electric Field Strength (E), Charge, Force.
Couple [N m] HL
A pair of equal and opposite parallel forces that tend to cause rotation only. The net force is zero, but the net torque is non-zero.
See also: Torque, Turning Moment, Force.
Crest
Top of a transverse wave.
See also: Trough, Transverse Wave, Amplitude.
Critical Damping
When a resistive force is applied to an oscillating system that causes the particle to return to zero displacement in a minimum amount of time.
Critical Density ρc [kg m⁻³] HL
The average density of matter in the universe required for it to eventually stop expanding and begin to contract. Determines the ultimate fate of the universe.
Critical Mass
Minimum mass of radioactive fuel block needed for a chain reaction to occur.
D
Damping
Involves a force that is always in the opposite direction to the direction of motion of the oscillating particle (a dissipative force).
Davisson-Germer Experiment HL
An experiment showing that electrons are scattered off crystals of nickel and interfere with each other – also known as 'electron diffraction.' This experiment is evidence for the existence of matter waves.
Decay Constant (λ) λ [s⁻¹]
Constant of proportionality between the decay rate (activity) and the number of radioactive nuclei present. OR Probability of decay of a particular nuclei per unit time.
See also: Radioactive Half-life (T₁/₂), Activity (A), Radioactive Decay Law.
Decimal Number
Number written using base-10.
Degraded Energy
In any process that involves energy transformations, the energy that is transferred to the surroundings (thermal energy) is no longer available to perform useful work.
Derived Units
Units that are combinations of fundamental units. These combinations may or may not have a separate name.
Destructive Interference
Superposition of two waves which are out of phase with each other.
See also: Constructive Interference, Principle of Superposition, Path Difference.
Diffraction
The bending of a wave around an obstacle or the spreading of a wave through an opening. Diffraction is only noticeable when the size of the opening is smaller than or on the same order of the size of the wavelength.
See also: Wavelength (λ), Resolution, Rayleigh Criterion.
Digital
Technique involving codes or signals made up of a large number of binary digits (bits) that can each take only one of two possible values.
See also: Analog, Bit, Byte.
Direction of a Magnetic Field
The direction that the North pole of a small test compass would point if placed in the field (N to S).
Dispersion
The phenomenon where the refractive index of a material varies with wavelength, causing different wavelengths of light to refract by different amounts. This causes white light to separate into its component colors.
See also: Refractive Index (n), Wavelength (λ), Scattering.
Displacement (for waves) x [m]
Distance a particle moves in a particular direction from its mean (equilibrium) position.
Displacement (s) s [m]
Distance traveled in a particular direction (change in position).
See also: Distance, Vector, Velocity (u,v).
Distance d [m]
The length of the path traveled by an object, a scalar quantity.
See also: Displacement (s), Scalar.
Doppler Effect
The change of frequency of a wave due to the movement of the source or the observer relative to the medium of wave transmission.
Dose Equivalent (H) H [Sv] HL
Product of quality factor and absorbed dose which is an attempt to measure the radiation damage that actually occurs in tissues.
E
Effective Half-life (TE) TE [s] HL
Time taken for the number of radioactive nuclei present in the body to halve.
Efficiency (eff) η [dimensionless or %]
The ratio of the useful energy (or power or work) output to the total energy (or power or work) input.
Einstein's Closed Elevator
An object dropped inside a closed elevator will accelerate toward the floor – could be explained in one of two ways: either the elevator is far from any planet but accelerating upward or the elevator is at rest on the surface of planet.
Elastic Collision
A collision in which kinetic energy is conserved.
See also: Inelastic Collision, Kinetic Energy (EK), Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum.
Electric Current (I) I [A]
Current is defined in terms of the force per unit length between parallel current-carrying conductors. One ampere of current is the amount of current in each of two infinitely long straight wires one meter apart experiencing a magnetic force per unit length of 2 x 10⁻⁷ newtons.
See also: Charge, Time, Ohm's Law, Resistance (R).
Electric Field Strength (E) E [N C⁻¹ or V m⁻¹]
Electric force per positive unit test charge (E = F/q).
See also: Force, Charge, Coulomb's Law, Electric Potential (V).
Electric Potential (V) V [V or J C⁻¹]
Work done per unit charge moving a small positive test charge in from infinity to a point in an electric field (V = W/q or V = kq/r). The work done is path independent.
See also: Electric Field Strength (E), Charge, Work (W), Electric Potential Difference (ΔV).
Electric Potential Difference (ΔV) ΔV [V]
Electric potential energy difference per unit charge between two points in an electric field (ΔV = ΔEe / q OR ΔV = W / q).
See also: Electric Potential (V), Electric Current (I), Resistance (R).
Electric Potential Energy (Ee) Ee [J]
Energy that a charge has due to its position in an electric field.
Electromagnetic Radiation
Energy propagated through space as oscillating electric and magnetic fields. Includes radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.
See also: Photon, Wave, Frequency (f), Wavelength (λ), Gamma Radiation.
Electromotive Force (emf) (ε) ε [V]
Total energy difference per unit charge around a circuit (total energy per unit charge made available by the chemical reaction in the battery) (ε = ΔEe/q OR ε = W/q).
See also: Electric Potential Difference (ΔV), Internal Resistance (r), Electric Current (I).
Electron e⁻
A negatively charged subatomic particle found in atoms.
Electron in a Box Model HL
A model of the atom useful for explaining the origin of atomic energy levels: The model assumes that, if an electron is confined to move in one dimension by a box, the de Broglie waves associated with the electron will be standing waves of wavelength 2L/n where L is the length of the box and n is a positive integer. Further, the kinetic energy of the electron in the box is n²h²/(8meL²).
Electronvolt (eV) eV [1.60×10⁻¹⁹ J]
Energy gained by an electron moving through an electric potential difference of one volt. OR Work done moving an electron through an electric potential difference of one volt (1 eV = 1.60 x 10⁻¹⁹ J).
Ellipse
A closed curve in which the sum of distances from any point on the curve to two fixed points (foci) is constant. Most planetary orbits are elliptical.
See also: Orbit, Foci.
Emissivity (ε) ε [dimensionless]
Ratio of power emitted by an object to the power emitted by a black-body at the same temperature.
See also: Albedo (α), Stefan-Boltzmann Law, Black-Body Radiation.
Energy E [J]
The capacity to do work, measured in joules (J).
See also: Work (W), Power (P), Kinetic Energy (EK), Principle of Conservation of Energy.
Energy Density (of a fuel) [J kg⁻¹]
The ratio of the energy released from the fuel to the mass of the fuel consumed.
Enhanced (Anthropogenic) Greenhouse Effect
Human activities, mainly related to the burning of fossil fuels, have released extra carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, thereby enhancing or amplifying the greenhouse effect (a possible cause of global warming).
Entropy S [J K⁻¹]
A system property that expresses the degree of disorder in the system.
See also: Second Law of Thermodynamics, Temperature (T).
Equilibrium
A state where all forces or influences are balanced.
Equipotential Surface
Every point on it has the same potential.
Escape Speed ve [m s⁻¹]
Minimum speed of a rocket needed to escape the gravitational attraction of a planet.
Evaporation
When faster moving molecules have enough energy to escape from the surface of a liquid that is at a temperature less than its boiling point, leaving slower moving molecules behind which results in a cooling of the liquid.
Exposure (X) X [C kg⁻¹] HL
Measure of the total amount of ionization produced which is the ratio of total charge to mass of air.
F
Faraday's Law
The emf induced by a time changing magnetic field is proportional to the rate of change of the flux linkage (ε α N ΔΦ/Δt).
See also: Magnetic Flux (Φ), Electromotive Force (emf) (ε), Lenz's Law.
Field
A region of space where a force can be detected.
First Law of Thermodynamics
The thermal energy transferred to a system from its surroundings is equal to the work done by the system plus the change in internal energy of the system (an application of the principle of conservation of energy).
See also: Internal Energy of a substance (U), Thermal Energy (Heat) (Q), Work (W), Principle of Conservation of Energy.
Flux Φ [W m⁻²] or [particles m⁻² s⁻¹]
The rate of flow of energy or particles through a surface per unit area. In physics, often refers to electromagnetic radiation energy passing through a unit area per unit time.
See also: Intensity (I), Solar Constant, Luminosity, Radiation.
Foci
The two fixed points that define an ellipse. In planetary orbits, the Sun occupies one focus. Also called focal points (singular: focus).
See also: Ellipse.
Force F [N]
A push or pull acting on an object, measured in newtons (N).
See also: Newton's Second Law of Motion, Mass, Acceleration (a), Work (W).
Forced Oscillations
A system may be forced to oscillate at any given frequency by an outside driving force that is applied to it.
Fossil Fuels
Coal, oil, and natural gas. Industrialization led to a high rate of energy usage leading to industry being developed near large deposits of fossil fuels.
Frame of Reference
The point of view of an observer or a coordinate system against which measurements are made consisting of x,y,z axes and a clock.
Frequency (f) f [Hz or s⁻¹]
Number of oscillations per unit time.
See also: Period (T), Wavelength (λ), Wave Speed (v), Angular Speed.
Fuel Enrichment
Process by which the percentage composition of a desirable radioactive nuclide (e.g., uranium-235) is increased in order to make nuclear fission more likely.
Fundamental (First Harmonic)
Lowest frequency mode of vibration of a standing wave.
Fundamental Units
Seven basic units of the SI measurement system: kilogram, second, mole, meter, ampere, Kelvin, candela.
G
Galaxy
A massive gravitationally bound system consisting of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas, dust, and dark matter. Examples include the Milky Way.
Galilean Transformation HL
Equations that translate measurements made in one frame of reference to another frame of reference without taking into account the theory of relativity.
Gamma Radiation γ
High energy electromagnetic radiation with the shortest wavelength and highest frequency in the electromagnetic spectrum. Emitted during radioactive decay and nuclear reactions.
Gamma Radiation (γ) γ
High energy (high frequency) electromagnetic radiation.
See also: Electromagnetic Radiation, Radioactive Decay, Photon.
Geiger-Marsden experiment
Also known as the Rutherford Alpha Particle Scattering or Gold Foil Experiment.
Global Warming
Increase in mean temperature of the Earth in recent years.
Gravitational Field Strength (g) g [N kg⁻¹ or m s⁻²]
Gravitational force per unit mass on a point mass (g = Fg / m).
See also: Force, Mass, Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation, Gravitational Potential Energy (EP).
Gravitational Mass mg [kg] HL
The property of an object that determines how much gravitational force it feels when near another object. Different gravitational masses have different gravitational forces acting between them.
Gravitational Potential (V) V [J kg⁻¹]
The work done per unit mass in moving a mass from infinity to a point in space.
Gravitational Potential Energy (EP) EP [J]
The work done in moving a mass from infinity to a point in space. The work done is path independent.
See also: Energy, Mass, Gravitational Field Strength (g), Work (W).
Gravitational Red Shift HL
A prediction of the general theory relativity in which clocks slow down in a gravitational field (that is, clocks on the ground floor of a building will run slowly as compared with clocks on the top floor).
Greenhouse Effect
Short wavelength radiation received from the Sun causes the Earth's surface to warm up. Earth will then emit longer wavelength radiation (infrared) which is absorbed by some gases (e.g., methane, water vapor, carbon dioxide, nitrous oxide) in the atmosphere and re-radiated in all directions. This extra warming of the Earth's atmosphere is known as the Greenhouse Effect.
See also: Albedo (α), Global Warming, Enhanced (Anthropogenic) Greenhouse Effect.
H
Hafele-Keating Experiment HL
A 1971 test of the predictions of time dilation in which atomic clocks were put in aircraft and flown, east and west, around the world and then compared with clocks that remained fixed in the same location on Earth.
Half-Value Thickness (x₁/₂) x₁/₂ [m] HL
The thickness of a material needed for a beam (of X-rays) to be attenuated (reduced) in intensity by 50%.
Heat Exchanger
This allows the nuclear reactions to occur in a place that is sealed off from the rest of the environment. Reactions increase temperature in the core and this thermal energy is transferred to water and the steam that is produced turns the turbines.
Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle HL
Conjugate quantities (position-momentum or time-energy) cannot be known precisely at the same time. There is a link between the uncertainty principle and the de Broglie hypothesis.
Helium Burning HL
Nuclear fusion process in which helium nuclei combine to form heavier elements, primarily carbon. Occurs in the core of stars after hydrogen is exhausted.
See also: Nuclear Fusion, Red Giant, Carbon-Oxygen Burning.
Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram H-R Diagram HL
A scatter plot of stars showing the relationship between their absolute magnitudes (or luminosities) and spectral types (or temperatures). Used to understand stellar evolution.
See also: Luminosity, Absolute Magnitude, Red Giant, White Dwarf, Blue Giant.
I
Ideal Ammeter
One with zero internal resistance – must be placed in series.
Ideal Gas
A gas that follows the ideal gas equation of state (PV = nRT) for all values of P, V, and T (an ideal gas cannot be liquefied).
See also: Real Gas, Pressure (P), Temperature (T).
Ideal Voltmeter
One with infinite internal resistance – must be placed in parallel.
Impulse (J) J [N s]
Change in momentum.
See also: Linear Momentum (p), Force, Time.
Inelastic Collision
A collision in which kinetic energy is not conserved.
See also: Elastic Collision, Kinetic Energy (EK), Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum.
Inertia
The resistance of an object to changes in its state of motion. A measure of how much an object resists acceleration when a force is applied.
See also: Mass, Newton's First Law of Motion.
Inertial Frame of Reference HL
A frame of reference that is not accelerating but is at rest or moving with a constant velocity. OR A frame of reference in which Newton's law of inertia is valid.
Inertial Mass mi [kg] HL
The property of an object that determines how much it accelerates when a given force is applied to it. OR The ratio of resultant force to acceleration of an object.
Inertial Mass-Energy E [J] HL
The total energy content of an object including its rest mass energy and kinetic energy, as described by Einstein's mass-energy equivalence.
See also: Rest Mass, Kinetic Energy (EK), Special Theory of Relativity.
Infrared Radiation IR
Electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths longer than visible light but shorter than microwaves. Associated with heat and thermal radiation.
See also: Thermal Radiation, Electromagnetic Radiation, Wavelength (λ).
Insulator
Material through which electric charge does not flow freely.
See also: Conductor, Electric Field Strength (E).
Intensity (I) I [W m⁻²]
Power received per unit area. For a wave, its intensity is proportional to the square of its amplitude.
See also: Flux, Power (P), Brightness, Luminosity.
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)
Panel established by the World Meteorological Organization and the United Nations Environment Programme in 1988 in which hundreds of governmental scientific representatives from more than one hundred countries regularly assess the up-to-date evidence from international research into global warming and human induced climate change.
Internal Energy of a substance (U) U [J]
The total potential energy and random kinetic energy of the molecules of the substance.
See also: Temperature (T), Thermal Energy (Heat) (Q), First Law of Thermodynamics.
Internal Resistance (r) r [Ω]
Resistance inside a battery that causes the battery's terminal potential difference to be less than its emf. Internal resistance in a meter causes it not to act as an ideal meter.
Isobaric
A process that occurs at constant pressure (ΔP = 0).
Isochoric (Isovolumetric)
A process that occurs at constant volume (ΔV = 0).
Isothermal
A process that occurs at constant temperature (ΔT = 0).
Isotope
Nuclei with the same number of protons (Z) but different number of neutrons (N).
K
Kelvin scale of Temperature [K]
An absolute scale of temperature in which 0 K is the absolute zero of temperature.
Kepler's Third Law
The ratio of the orbital period squared to the average orbital radius cubed is constant for all planets.
Kinetic Energy (EK) EK [J]
Product of ½ times the mass of an object times the square of an object's speed.
See also: Energy, Mass, Velocity (u,v), Work (W).
Kyoto Protocol
An amendment to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change in which signatory countries agree to work towards achieving a stipulated reduction in greenhouse gas emissions.
L
Laminar Flow
Smooth, orderly fluid flow in which fluid particles move in parallel layers with no mixing between layers. Occurs at low Reynolds numbers.
See also: Turbulence, Stokes Law.
Law of Conservation of Charge
The total electric charge of an isolated system remains constant.
Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum
The total momentum of an isolated system (no external forces) remains constant.
See also: Linear Momentum (p).
Law of Reflection
The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection when both angles are measured with respect to the normal line.
Least-Significant Bit (LSB)
Right hand digit representing the smallest power.
Length Contraction HL
An effect of relativity in which the separation between two points in space contracts if there is relative motion in that direction.
See also: Special Theory of Relativity, Proper Length, Lorentz Factor (γ).
Lenz's Law
The direction of an induced emf is such that it produces a magnetic field whose direction opposes the change in magnetic field that produced it (ε = - N ΔΦ/Δt).
See also: Faraday's Law, Magnetic Flux (Φ).
Light Clock HL
A beam of light reflected between two parallel mirrors used to measure time.
Light-Dependent Resistor (LDR)
Sensor whose resistance depends on amount of light shining on its surface – increase in light causes a decrease in resistance.
Linear Momentum (p) p [kg m s⁻¹ or N s]
Product of mass and velocity.
See also: Mass, Velocity (u,v), Impulse (J), Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum.
Longitudinal Wave
Wave in which the direction of motion of the energy transfer (the wave) is parallel to the direction of motion of the particles of the medium. Sound waves are longitudinal.
See also: Transverse Wave, Compression, Rarefaction.
Lorentz Factor (γ) γ [dimensionless] HL
A relativistic factor that varies from approximately 1 at low velocities to approaching infinity near the speed of light.
See also: Special Theory of Relativity, Time Dilation, Length Contraction.
Loudness
The response of the ear to intensity.
Luminosity L [W]
The total amount of energy emitted by a star or other astronomical object per unit time. The absolute brightness of a star.
See also: Power (P), Brightness, Absolute Magnitude, Stefan-Boltzmann Law.
M
Magnetic Flux (Φ) Φ [Wb or T m²]
Product of the magnetic field strength and a cross-sectional area and the cosine of the angle between the magnetic field and the normal to the area (Φ = B A cosθ).
See also: Magnitude of a Magnetic Field (B), Faraday's Law, Magnetic Flux Linkage.
Magnetic Flux Linkage NΦ [Wb]
Product of the magnetic flux through a single coil and the total number of coils (flux linkage = N Φ).
Magnification m [dimensionless]
Ratio of the length of the image on the CCD to the length of the object.
Magnitude of a Magnetic Field (B) B [T or Wb m⁻²]
Ratio of magnetic force on a current carrying conductor to the product of the current and length of wire and sine of the angle between the current and the magnetic field (B = FB / Ilsinθ). OR Ratio of magnetic force on a charged particle to the product of the charge and its velocity and the sine of the angle between the velocity and the magnetic field (B = FB / qvsinθ).
See also: Force, Magnetic Flux (Φ), Faraday's Law.
Malus' Law
The transmitted intensity of polarized light is equal to the product of the incident intensity times the square of the cosine of the angle between the direction of the analyzer and the direction of the electric field vibration of the polarized light (I = Io cos² θ).
Mass m [kg]
The amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms (kg).
See also: Force, Inertia, Gravitational Mass, Inertial Mass.
Mass Defect Δm [kg]
Difference between the mass of the nucleus and the sum of the masses of its individual nucleons.
See also: Binding Energy, Unified Atomic Mass Unit, Energy.
Matter Waves HL
All moving particles have a 'matter wave' associated with them whose wavelength is the de Broglie wavelength.
See also: de Broglie Hypothesis, Wave-Particle Duality, Electron.
Medium
The substance through which a wave travels.
Michelson-Morley Experiment HL
An experiment comparing the speed of light measured in two perpendicular directions using interference patterns. Did not produce any observable difference in the speeds – implies that the ether does not exist and that the result is consistent with the constancy of the speed of light.
Micrometer Screw Gauge [accuracy: ~0.01 mm]
A precision instrument for measuring small distances or thicknesses, typically accurate to 0.01 mm, using a calibrated screw mechanism.
See also: Precision.
Millikan's Stopping Potential Experiment HL

An object at rest remains at rest and an object in motion remains in motion at a constant speed in a straight line unless acted on by an unbalanced force.

An unbalanced force will cause an object to accelerate in the direction of the net force. The acceleration of the object is proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to its mass.

Newton's Third Law of Motion
When two bodies A and B interact (push or pull), the force that A exerts on B is equal and opposite to the force that B exerts on A.
See also: Newton's First Law of Motion, Newton's Second Law of Motion, Force.

The force of gravity between two objects is directly proportional to the product of the two masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them and acts along a line joining their centers.
